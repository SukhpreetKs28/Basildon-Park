Crimson Room

Back to Main Page Go to Second Floor

After following the stairs of the Grand Staircase and gallery path, you come face to face with the Crimson Room, another example of the house's repetitive use of red, echoing an 18th-century style that the Iliffes have brought in their restoration efforts. The Bedroom is located in the range of bedrooms and dressing rooms on the south side of the house, described by Papworth’s plans as the ‘family side’, and they were served via the side stairs. Whereas the rooms located to the north of the house were a series of guest apartments known as the ‘Regent’s side’, referring to a princely visit that was paid in 1813 to the 3rd Sir Francis Sykes.

The room was unfinished when the Sykes’ lived at Basildon, though the ceiling and cornices are likely to be designed by John Carr, with a daisy pattern being a prominent element in the frieze. Inside the 1829 sale catalogue, there is said to be ‘Indian Paper’ on the walls, which is likely to have been obtained by Sykes during his outpost with the East India Company, but by the time the Iliffes owned the house, this wallpaper was lost. Papworth originally designed this room as Mary Ann Morrison’s boudoir/sitting room while her husband occupied the rooms below, such as the now 1950s kitchen. This left her in charge of the upper floor, where she and her children slept, thereby giving the ‘family side’ of the house a certain kind of matriarchy.

After the war, this went through a kind of furnishings transformation at the hands of Lady Iliffe. Many of the rooms on this floor had retained their original cornices, except doors, doorcases, and fire-surrounds were missing when the Iliffes moved in. This is possibly the result of the army’s occupation of the home or due to Ferdinando, who sold many of these kinds of things in America. Replacements came from Panton Hall, also designed by Carr, which ended up ‘fitted like a glove’. The wallpaper by Coles was hung in the 1950s, and Lady Illiffe added her own personal touch with a rug incorporating both flowers and shrubs from the gardens at Basildon in 1956, which she handmade and has her initials engraved in the corner.

She also managed to purchase both the curtains and the furniture at the Ashburnham sale, featuring the bed, which was possibly made by George Morant, a Royal Upholsterer, in 1930 and is one of the best surviving examples of William IV-style upholstery, transforming the room into the now crimson room. The state bed that gives the room its name is from a suite of furniture made in 1829 for the 3rd Earl of Ashburnham and is rumoured to have been made for a Royal visit by William IV. The Ashburnham sale is known to be one of the most notorious and biggest of the country house sales in the period, with Lady Iliffe purchasing the whole set for only £100. To make the home more suited to 1950s living, the Iliffes converted some of the smaller bedrooms into suite bathrooms.

Interpretations for this room show consideration of the space, where aspects like privacy and status intersect within the context of country life, and explore how bedrooms can often reveal changing attitudes towards domestic life and gender across different historical periods. This room's restoration leads to questions surrounding how 'historic' interiors are selectively recreated for a modern audience and use.
