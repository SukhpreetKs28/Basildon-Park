Basildon House has had a long list of owners, and this in turn has led to its being altered and reconstructed many times throughout its life. The earliest known reference to Basildon was in 1311, when Elias de Coleshulle and his heirs were granted free warren over Coleshill, Buscot and Basildon. In the 1600s and 1700s, merchants from Bristol, known as the Yonge Family, owned the manor of Basildon, which was then passed to Henry Fane and his heirs in the 1680s after the death of the 5th Earl of Bath, Henry Bourchier, who had no male heir of his own. From these owners, only some of the Gothic lodges at the entrances to the south and north drives, built during the time of the 2nd and last Viscount Fane, have survived. Some of these Oxford lodges were located in the north direction of the current house, with a stable attached. Between 1720-40, Lady Fane built a grotto on the estate, and the parts not demolished are still attached to the house, although the National Trust does not own them. It was only after the death of the 2nd Viscount of Fane in 1766 that the house fell into the hands of the sisters, who sold it to Sykes in 1770.

In 1777, Sykes, a newly wealthy ‘nabob’ recently returned from the East India Company and looking to realise his political aspirations, bought Basildon Park and its 2,500 acres, and so an executive decision was made to build a modern Palladian-style house overlooking the River Thames within a large landscape garden. The Palladian style is inspired by the north Italian architect Andrea Palladio, favouring the plainness, symmetry and proportion that was popular in British country houses in the 18th Century. Work was still being undertaken in 1794, ‘gradually completing in a style of elegance suited to the exterior appearance’, with some rooms never being fully furnished due to monetary troubles that left the house mortgaged. The estate was left to the 3rd Baronet, who was an avid gambler, and with expensive court dealings, his gambling, his family's resources were rapidly depleting, causing Basildon to become subject to letting and eventually led to being sold for £97,000 to the art collector and Liberal MP James Morrison in 1838.

Morrison was an old-fashioned self-made man who began his career in a Haberdashery. He first made his fortune as a partner in his father-in-law’s Haberdashery business, and by 1830 he was the sole owner, increasing his fortune to 2 billion pounds, which he invested in North American and French railways, furthering his wealth. When he purchased Basildon house, Morrison brought his collection of Old Master and contemporary paintings, which allowed him to display his wealth and prizes, ‘What a casket to enclose pictorial gems’. By employing Papworth, Morrison was able to complete the interior furnishings of the house and construct other estate buildings.

The house remained in this family until 1929, when it was used as a hospital for the Grenadier Guards in WW1. In that year, Charles Morrisson, James' son, sold the estate after the failure of his marriage, and Edward Iliffe purchased it. He kept the land which bordered his own property in Yattendon and removed seven doors and two chimney pieces for his own London home, then sold the estate to George Ferdinando, a property developer who was interested in selling the fixtures and fittings of the house before he demolished it. The ceiling roundels and fireplace were sold to the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in New York. He attempted to sell it to the US but received no bidders and instead decided to attempt to restore it until WW2 occurred, and it was requisitioned and then destroyed by the army troops.

The Iliffe’s purchased Basildon in 1952 with the idea of saving it, ‘I remember saying to a friend, “How sad what a waste – and it could still be saved” and he said, “Why don’t you?”,’ (Lady Iliffe), drawn in by atmosphere of fine elegance that the house had managed to retain despite its derelict state as well as their shared love for the Georgian period’s art and architecture. The Iliffes restored the house over 25 years while war rationing was still in effect, and they had to take repairs slowly. They properly and even looked at Carr’s other work to make the house like it once was, even by purchasing doors that were in other houses he designed, as well as attending multiple country house estate sales to furnish the house and created a collection of 17th and 18th century European artwork that fit their narrative of the house. It was in 1978 that the Iliffes donated the house to the National Trust and much of its collections, and they remained living in the South Pavilion until their deaths.

After taking over the house, the National Trust has put in time to install projects that relate to the owners of the house, such as the Shell room and Sutherland Room and obtain Sykes Chinese dinner service plates that were sold off long ago, even cutlery that was found in the Netherlands through the use of sponsorship via donations from the Iliffes and the Art Fund. It is in recent years that the Trust has started to use the Park’s links to India to celebrate Diwali and bring in local artists to create within the house for entertainment, as well as to produce exhibitions that explore the links between India and Berkshire over the last 250 years.
