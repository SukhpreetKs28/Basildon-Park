Green Chintz Room

This room is a mix of modern and old furnishings from a room on the Regent’s side of the house. Named after the hanging on the domed bed that was originally hung with green chintz, whereas now only remnants can be seen through the bed linings that are composed of the original chintz and material from the window curtains. The curtains surrounding the bed have had a glaze on them that, although faded from sunlight, has largely retained its colour.

The lit a la Polonaise (hangings on the domed bed) itself came from the Ditchley Park sale in Oxfordshire and was made in 1790 for the 12th Viscount Dillon, with which this guest bedroom is closely associated, as Lady Iliffe’s sister, Irene, is married to the 20th Viscount Dillon and was continuously used by her and their 9 children. They continued to use this room even after the National Trust acquired the house from the Iliffes, leaving their mark throughout the room, such as through the many portraits and paintings of the Dillion family, as well as the more modern aspects of their lives, like the 1950’s tea maker on their bedside table. The paintings are on loan to the National Trust, featuring the descendants of the first owner of the bed.

Interpretations should focus on how the space shows how imported fabrics and decorative fashions have shaped the domestic interiors, with the chintz textiles allowing visitors to explore the connections between British country house culture and colonial trade, like the importation of patterned cottons from India that are symbols of refinement and status. This should encourage visitors to question how heritage spaces reconstruct comfort and authenticity for their modern audiences.
