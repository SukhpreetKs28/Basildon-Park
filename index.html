What is it?

Basildon Park, located between the villages of Upper Basildon and Lower Basildon in Berkshire, England, is a National Trust property made popular by its appearance in iconic films and Shows such as Bridgeton and the 2005 Pride & Prejudice. Sitting in 400 acres of historic parkland and gardens stretching towards the river Thames, this 18th Century Palladian mansion that was built up this ancient estate using Neo-classical architecture between 1777-83 through the fortune of Sir Francis Sykes from his service in the East India Company, and through the subsequent years has fallen into the hands of a multitude of owners who have all adapted and added to this house either from their own collections or treasures procured from other houses.

When you reach the house itself after a trip through the woodland, you are met with an exceedingly beautiful sight, where ‘every hill is covered with noble beech woods, and the River Thames runs through the meadows of the Front of the house’ (Amelia Carr, 1786). The West Front of the house was situated by the architect John Carr to overlook the Thames to the east and the parkland to the west, with the house’s colouring weathering from golden masonry to creamy silver, enhancing the visual effect of the mansion. It features an Ionic portico that creates an interplay between light and dark and is flanked by two pavilions that create four separate courtyards used to hide the servants from view, with the South pavilion used to house Lord and Lady Illiffe after the house was given to the National Trust. The East Front extends from the pavilions to showcase the curated Gardens and overlook the River Thames.

The house is designed to reflect the wealth, status, and classical taste of its owner and to embody the architectural ideals associated with elite Georgian society. Its symmetrical design, formal interiors and landscape setting show its status as both a domestic residence and a public statement of power and refinement. The mansion was requisitioned during both World Wars and was later bought by Lord and Lady Illiffe in the 1950s, who renovated and restored the interior over 25 years, after which they gave it to the National Trust to care for.

The Mansion houses a collection of 17th–20th-century English and European paintings, including a series paintings of the Apostles by Batoni and a display in the Sutherland Room. The furniture is mainly from the 18th century, with an English and European background, and is featured predominantly in the Entrance Hall and the Crimson Bedroom. However, the oldest piece in the house predates the house itself: a 13th-century ceramic Yingqing Vase, now used as a lamp.

As a heritage site open to the public, Basildon Park offers a curated historical experience seen by the effort put in by the national trust and its many Volunteers to make decisions of the restorations, the presentation of the rooms and interpretations and how to help visitor engagement to shape the history of the house often amplifying certain parts of history such as the wealthy elite status of the house while shielding elements such as military use and aspects such as servant labour and colonial wealth to protect its image that has been tryingly preserved throughout the years. This site takes you through the history and reconstruction of the house and its portrayal as a National Trust site.


Reconstruction & Key Owners

Basildon House has had a long list of owners, and this in turn has led to its being altered and reconstructed many times throughout its life. The earliest known reference to Basildon was in 1311, when Elias de Coleshulle and his heirs were granted free warren over Coleshill, Buscot and Basildon. In the 1600s and 1700s, merchants from Bristol, known as the Yonge Family, owned the manor of Basildon, which was then passed to Henry Fane and his heirs in the 1680s after the death of the 5th Earl of Bath, Henry Bourchier, who had no male heir of his own. From these owners, only some of the Gothic lodges at the entrances to the south and north drives, built during the time of the 2nd and last Viscount Fane, have survived. Some of these Oxford lodges were located in the north direction of the current house, with a stable attached. Between 1720-40, Lady Fane built a grotto on the estate, and the parts not demolished are still attached to the house, although the National Trust does not own them. It was only after the death of the 2nd Viscount of Fane in 1766 that the house fell into the hands of the sisters, who sold it to Sykes in 1770.

In 1777, Sykes, a newly wealthy ‘nabob’ recently returned from the East India Company and looking to realise his political aspirations, bought Basildon Park and its 2,500 acres, and so an executive decision was made to build a modern Palladian-style house overlooking the River Thames within a large landscape garden. The Palladian style is inspired by the north Italian architect Andrea Palladio, favouring the plainness, symmetry and proportion that was popular in British country houses in the 18th Century. Work was still being undertaken in 1794, ‘gradually completing in a style of elegance suited to the exterior appearance’, with some rooms never being fully furnished due to monetary troubles that left the house mortgaged. The estate was left to the 3rd Baronet, who was an avid gambler, and with expensive court dealings, his gambling, his family's resources were rapidly depleting, causing Basildon to become subject to letting and eventually led to being sold for £97,000 to the art collector and Liberal MP James Morrison in 1838.

Morrison was an old-fashioned self-made man who began his career in a Haberdashery. He first made his fortune as a partner in his father-in-law’s Haberdashery business, and by 1830 he was the sole owner, increasing his fortune to 2 billion pounds, which he invested in North American and French railways, furthering his wealth. When he purchased Basildon house, Morrison brought his collection of Old Master and contemporary paintings, which allowed him to display his wealth and prizes, ‘What a casket to enclose pictorial gems’. By employing Papworth, Morrison was able to complete the interior furnishings of the house and construct other estate buildings.

The house remained in this family until 1929, when it was used as a hospital for the Grenadier Guards in WW1. In that year, Charles Morrisson, James' son, sold the estate after the failure of his marriage, and Edward Iliffe purchased it. He kept the land which bordered his own property in Yattendon and removed seven doors and two chimney pieces for his own London home, then sold the estate to George Ferdinando, a property developer who was interested in selling the fixtures and fittings of the house before he demolished it. The ceiling roundels and fireplace were sold to the Waldorf Astoria Hotel in New York. He attempted to sell it to the US but received no bidders and instead decided to attempt to restore it until WW2 occurred, and it was requisitioned and then destroyed by the army troops.

The Iliffe’s purchased Basildon in 1952 with the idea of saving it, ‘I remember saying to a friend, “How sad what a waste – and it could still be saved” and he said, “Why don’t you?”,’ (Lady Iliffe), drawn in by atmosphere of fine elegance that the house had managed to retain despite its derelict state as well as their shared love for the Georgian period’s art and architecture. The Iliffes restored the house over 25 years while war rationing was still in effect, and they had to take repairs slowly. They properly and even looked at Carr’s other work to make the house like it once was, even by purchasing doors that were in other houses he designed, as well as attending multiple country house estate sales to furnish the house and created a collection of 17th and 18th century European artwork that fit their narrative of the house. It was in 1978 that the Iliffes donated the house to the National Trust and much of its collections, and they remained living in the South Pavilion until their deaths.

After taking over the house, the National Trust has put in time to install projects that relate to the owners of the house, such as the Shell room and Sutherland Room and obtain Sykes Chinese dinner service plates that were sold off long ago, even cutlery that was found in the Netherlands through the use of sponsorship via donations from the Iliffes and the Art Fund. It is in recent years that the Trust has started to use the Park’s links to India to celebrate Diwali and bring in local artists to create within the house for entertainment, as well as to produce exhibitions that explore the links between India and Berkshire over the last 250 years.

The Park Land & Gardens

Set in 400 acres of parkland, Basildon Park offers a range of activities to keep you on your toes, such as taking one of four parkland walks, easily marked to follow, that let you experience something new and different each time. Through the walks, you can discover the Nissen huts built during WW2 to house prisoners of war, see the changing colours the landscape offers, and explore the historic trees and picnic spots the park has to offer. The gardens are another aspect of Basildon that is a must-see and explore the layered history of the gardens as they went through their own state of ruin and restoration. Explore the pleasure ground designed by David Brandon in the 1800s and see Lady Iliffe’s rose gardens bloom during Spring and Summer, as well as look over the Italianate terrace that encloses the lawn and offers views over the Thames valley hills.

When Sir Francis Sykes was the owner, the park came up to the east side of the house in the approved ‘natural landscape’ manner. It was in the 19th century that Papworth and Brandon provided designs for the gardens and parks marking the shift to a more ‘manufactured’ natural landscape. When the Iliffes moved to Basildon, the garden and park were left in a derelict state, much like the house. Wartime acquisition sealed the fate of the garden as tank drivers trained for battle in the park, with prisoners of war being held in Nissen huts on the parkland, and the trees being used as practice to hide behind to escape bullets. The Iliffes immediately set to work repairing the landscape from the aftermath of neglect and ruin that led to over-growth, tank tracks, broken trees, broken architecture, as well as the mess the army left behind in the form of barbed wire, oil drums and the Nissen huts that can still be seen on the parkland walks. Between 1952 and 1955, the area around the house was cleared and lawns were re-seeded, and by 1964, the courtyards were paved with brick. In 1967, the pleasure grounds were cleared and replanted to restore the few surviving remnants of the original shrubbery. In the 1960s, landscape architect Lanning Roper was commissioned to create a small garden in front of the South Pavilion for Lady Iliffe, which later became their personal garden when they formally moved into the South Pavilion and was known as her Rose Garden. The terrace outside the East Front is a lawn originally planted as a parterre to provide a view of the Thames Valley. The terrace is a vital feature of the Park as it acts as a uniting force between the house, garden and park, linking all three spatially, metaphorically.

The Oxford Lodges is the first thing you see when you enter the Basildon estate and leaves an impression of a grand place with considerable wealth backing it, showing off the owners at the time, Sykes and later Morrison, who hoped to achieve with it. While Carr designed it, Morrison built upon his design to add more height, making the gestures more imposing in the eyes of outsiders, as well as adding ornaments to showcase his wealth and status. After passing these gates, you reach the Old 19th-century Frame yard, now used as a car park, but used to contain glasshouses devoted to growing fruits and flowers. The old kitchen garden on the opposing side of the red-brick wall and the orangery built by Papworth are both privately owned. The Stables yard is the last part you see before you can reach the house. While Sykes's stable was in front of the house, it was demolished by Morrison as it blocked the view of the park from the Loggia and was moved and used by the National Trust staff.

The park is first recorded visually by John Roque through a map in 1762 showing the Fanes’ manor house surrounded by a small formal garden within a deer park. Sykes, Morrison and the Iliffes have been carrying around new planting schemes to add and remove paths and garden ornaments, forming, abandoning and then restoring walled gardens. The park was in a state of neglect when Morrison purchased it. In 1894, Papworth advised Morrison that the house should not sit isolated in an expanse of lawn but blend with its surroundings. Basildon Park has become more naturalistic in its planting in recent years, encouraging the spread of wild orchids in the summer. The parkland and gardens function not only as decorative surroundings of the house, but also as an historical landscape that portrays the changing attitudes towards taste, class, leisure and conservation across different centuries.



Military Use

Basildon was turned into a 50-bed convalescent home during World War One, housing officers of the Grenadier Guards and the White House in Lower Basildon, which housed local men of all ranks, with a training centre being established in the park for disabled soldiers and sailors to learn various trades. Patients' records can be found in the National and local archives for those injured who stayed at the House during this time. 60 members of the Women’s Land Army ran the estate, producing a large number of goods for consumption in the hospitals; they were also involved in charcoal burning and brickmaking. Archie Morrison, who returned to active duty as a Grenadier Guards Captain, supported these ventures with his personal fortune, but it was after the failure of his marriage that he sold the contents and, in 1929, the estate.

During WW2, Basildon was requisitioned in 1940, with Canadian soldiers arriving first, where they were seen shooting at tree trunks to know ‘how big a tree they had to get behind before they were safe from a bullet’. The house was used as the soldiers' training ground and suffered extensive damage to its interiors and grounds, most notably the fire that broke out in what was the Mess Room or, as we know it, Lady Iliffe’s Bedroom. The house served as a base for Easy Company of the 101st Airborne Division (the legendary “Band of Brothers”) as they prepared for D-Day. Some of the damage left behind included graffiti on walls, with messages, drawings, and names of those who would be heading to Normandy. While this was restored, the National Trust preserved some examples as a reminder and proof of this period in the house's history.

After the Americans left in 1944, the house was taken over by officers in charge of prisoners of war who were billeted in huts in the woodland, and was later used to house labourers working on the construction of the Harwell Atomic Energy Research Establishment. Ferdinando’s children attempted to carry out some repairs after his death, but it was clear it was a rapidly losing battle, so after the house was de-requisitioned in 1952, it was taken over by the Iliffe family. By the time they departed, almost every window was broken, lead had been stripped from the roof, causing a collapse in the Staircase Hall, and many of the interior and architectural features had been destroyed, in addition to the damage the parkland suffered as it was used for tank training and other training exercises. This period of the house's history marked the shift from representing elite domestic life to serving as a home for soldiers grappling with death. The repurposing of the house also showed how its functional adaptability can be replicated in interpretations of the heritage to echo these sentiments.


Prominence

Basildon Park is famous for being one of the few houses of its kind to survive the impact of the 20th century on aristocratic life and is now a popular filming location and tourist attraction, finding new fame as a popular filming location. Its preserved Georgian interiors have made it ideal for period dramas, appearing as Netherfield Park in Pride & Prejudice (2005) and as Lady Grantham’s London residence, Grantham House, in Downton Abbey. The house has also featured in The Duchess and Marie Antoinette; its restorative efforts to preserve the 18th century make it a perfect place for a historical setting.

    • When filming films and TV shows, it is important to protect the house from any damage or changes, which is why special effects, camera angles, and tricks are used. The 2005 Pride and Prejudice was one of the most ambitious shoots undertaken by the National Trust. The camera follows multiple guests at the Netherfield Ball through a series of interlocking rooms, all in one shot, changing direction to follow different guests. To protect any furniture that couldn’t be moved, ornate mirrors were protected in situ and then hidden behind prop walls; rooms within rooms were built; and lighting was hidden in false columns. To remove one carpet took twenty people and a scissor lift that took it out of the Loggia, and pieces of ‘flattage’ were used to protect items that couldn't be moved and were used to transform the Octagon Room. In Bridgerton, where the filming was centred in the garden, the ‘greens’ department brought 5,000 artificial flowers that transformed the space to create a backdrop to transform the scene. In addition to this, there were strict rules on lighting, with one side of the house having a complete ban due to being home to nests of bats.

    These use of techniques to protect the house is essential as it also means that it is safe to film at the historical houses causing them to gain more recognition in popular culture which would in turn increase the value of sites and the number of tourists as can be seen on the Ground Floor of Basildon House where there is an entire room dedicated to the filming and shoots that have occurred on the site.
