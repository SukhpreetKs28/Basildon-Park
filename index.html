What is it?

Basildon Park, located between the villages of Upper Basildon and Lower Basildon in Berkshire, England, is a National Trust property made popular by its appearance in iconic films and Shows such as Bridgeton and the 2005 Pride & Prejudice. Sitting in 400 acres of historic parkland and gardens stretching towards the river Thames, this 18th Century Palladian mansion that was built up this ancient estate using Neo-classical architecture between 1777-83 through the fortune of Sir Francis Sykes from his service in the East India Company, and through the subsequent years has fallen into the hands of a multitude of owners who have all adapted and added to this house either from their own collections or treasures procured from other houses.
When you reach the house itself after a trip through the woodland, you are met with an exceedingly beautiful sight, where ‘every hill is covered with noble beech woods, and the River Thames runs through the meadows of the Front of the house’ (Amelia Carr, 1786). The West Front of the house was situated by the architect John Carr to overlook the Thames to the east and the parkland to the west, with the house’s colouring weathering from golden masonry to creamy silver, enhancing the visual effect of the mansion. It features an Ionic portico that creates an interplay between light and dark and is flanked by two pavilions that create four separate courtyards used to hide the servants from view, with the South pavilion used to house Lord and Lady Illiffe after the house was given to the National Trust. The East Front extends from the pavilions to showcase the curated Gardens and overlook the River Thames.
The house is designed to reflect the wealth, status, and classical taste of its owner and to embody the architectural ideals associated with elite Georgian society. Its symmetrical design, formal interiors and landscape setting show its status as both a domestic residence and a public statement of power and refinement. The mansion was requisitioned during both World Wars and was later bought by Lord and Lady Illiffe in the 1950s, who renovated and restored the interior over 25 years, after which they gave it to the National Trust to care for.
The Mansion houses a collection of 17th–20th-century English and European paintings, including a series of Apostles paintings by Batoni and a display in the Sutherland Room. The furniture is mainly from the 18th century, with an English and European background, and is featured predominantly in the Entrance Hall and the Crimson Bedroom. However, the oldest piece in the house predates the house itself: a 13th-century ceramic Yingqing Vase, now used as a lamp.
As a heritage site open to the public, Basildon Park offers a curated historical experience seen by the effort put in by the national trust and its many Volunteers to make decisions of the restorations, the presentation of the rooms and interpretations and how to help visitor engagement to shape the history of the house often amplifying certain parts of history such as the wealthy elite status of the house while shielding elements such as military use and aspects such as servant labour and colonial wealth to protect its image that has been tryingly preserved throughout the years. This site takes you through the history and reconstruction of the house and its portrayal as a National Trust site.
