When you finally exit the Entrance Hall to venture deeper into the house, you are met with a grand staircase in a hall that is full of oddities. This forms a link for the majority of the house and is regarded as one of Carr’s most monumental interiors, using a skilful handling of both architecture, space and light. The stone staircase is anchored to the wall, reaching the upper floor to a galleried landing. Looking up from below, you can marvel at the uses of archways and the small glances you can catch of the rooms upstairs. The stairs themselves are decorated with medallions of putti and different classical figures in lead, replicating the staircase at Heveningham Hall. During restoration work in the 1950s, the missing and damaged sections were replaced using aluminium to make casts for the new medallions.

On the ground floor of this room, you can see multiple doors, many of which lead nowhere but are included to follow the architectural ideal of symmetry, a recurring theme throughout the house. Above each door, there are Neoclassical plasterwork themes that still survive from Carr but are now painted a cool green. The room also features a grand piano and a display case displaying the leftover rubble and surviving elements of the staircase hall's ceiling. The roof's collapse was due to a Ministry of Works caretaker in the late 1940s stealing the lead from the staircase roof, leading to the timbers being exposed to rot and the roof therefore collapsing. In response to the Ministry, the Hall was permanently altered by replacing the roof with a new flat ceiling rather than Carr’s original dome ceiling. Other features include a lantern that hangs in the middle of the hall, which came from Overton Park in October 1830 and is supported by a chain that allows it to be lowered for cleaning, as well as Adam-style painted pedestals that rest on the staircase, which came from Brockenhurst Park. When the National Trust took over the house in 1978, the galleries required strengthening, and so supporting brackets were recast from an original design that was found by Lord Iliffe.

At the time of building the house, Sykes’ finances were becoming limited, and so such a staircase could be considered to be an unnecessary expense and was therefore omitted to be built at first. As visitors did not require access to the bedrooms from the reception room, the galleries were designed to run around this hall, and any access needed upstairs could be done using the back staircase, while servants used the spiral staircase hidden from view. The idea that the staircase was added later is supported by the fact that the plasterwork above one door is disrupted by the staircase, throwing off the aesthetics of a neoclassical interior where symmetry is key. Interpretations of this room should discuss how it functions as a transitional space that portrays the social hierarchy and performance of the house, with the stairs serving as a design meant to impress visitors and impose the wealth of the elite on them through sheer size, decoration, and symmetry. There could also be attention drawn to how servants moved throughout the house so as not to seem to be perceived by the other class. The restored features of this hall raise questions about how restoration can blur the line between the preservation of authenticity and the carefully recreated heritage.
